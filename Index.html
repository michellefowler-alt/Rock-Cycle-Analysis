Questions — use the data tables to answer

1 Sample A has tiny holes (vesicles) and a glassy surface. What does this tell you about how fast it cooled and where cooling happened? Use the depth/temperature table to support your answer.
Fast cooling = very small or no crystals = glassy texture. The vesicles (holes) show gases were trapped before they could escape — this happens when lava cools quickly at the surface. The temperature table shows the surface is only 25°C, a huge drop from magma temperatures.
2 Samples A and D are both igneous rocks, but they look very different. Using the depth and temperature data, explain why Sample D has large crystals while Sample A does not.
Sample D cooled slowly 3–5 km underground at 200–400°C — slow cooling gives minerals time to grow into large crystals. Sample A cooled quickly at the surface — fast cooling means crystals barely have time to form, so the texture is fine-grained or glassy.
3 What two conditions (shown in the data table) changed the original rock into Sample C? Why did the rock change without melting?
Heat (500–700°C) and very high pressure at 10–15 km deep. The temperature was hot enough to rearrange minerals but not hot enough to melt the rock completely — that is exactly what metamorphism is.

Sample A — volcanic rock (basalt)

Mantle rock →partial melting→ Magma →eruption + fast cooling→ Volcanic igneous rock
4 What energy source causes the mantle rock to melt? Could this process happen without that energy source? Explain.
Earth's internal heat (also called geothermal energy) — this comes from radioactive materials decaying deep inside Earth and leftover heat from Earth's formation. Without it, the temperature underground would never get hot enough to melt rock, so no magma, no volcanoes, and no igneous rocks from cooling magma.

Sample B — layered rock (sandstone)

Existing rock →weathering + erosion→ Loose sediment →piling up + squeezing + cementing→ Sedimentary rock
5 The wavy patterns (cross-bedding) in Sample B show that water carried the sediment grains. What energy source ultimately powers the rivers and streams that moved those grains?
Solar energy (energy from the Sun) powers the water cycle — the Sun heats water, causing it to evaporate, form clouds, and fall as rain. That rainwater flows downhill into rivers, which carry and deposit sediment. Gravity also plays a role in pulling water and sediment downhill.

Sample C — banded rock (schist)

Existing rock →gets buried deep / subduction→ Intense heat + pressure (no melting) →minerals rearrange→ Metamorphic rock
6 Sample C originally formed 10–15 km underground but was found at the surface. What process brought it back up, and what energy source drives that process?
Tectonic uplift — Earth's internal heat drives convection currents in the mantle (think of slow-moving currents in thick liquid), which push tectonic plates around. When plates collide or separate, deep rocks can get pushed toward the surface. Over millions of years, the rock above erodes away, exposing what was once deep underground.

7 Imagine Earth's internal heat completely shut off. Which parts of the rock cycle would stop working? Which parts could still happen, at least for a while?
Without internal heat: no more volcanism, no plate movement, no metamorphism, no new magma. Surface processes driven by the Sun — weathering, erosion, and deposition — would continue as long as there is still relief (hills and mountains to erode). Eventually everything would flatten and the rock cycle would basically stop.
8 Scientists say the rock cycle recycles matter (the stuff rocks are made of) but does NOT recycle energy. Using examples from the samples, explain what that means in your own words.
Matter is recycled — the same atoms that were in Sample A's lava could end up in Sample B as sediment after weathering. But energy is not recycled — heat from Earth's interior and energy from the Sun have to keep coming in from outside the rock cycle to keep it running. Energy is used up, not passed back around.

Going further — critical thinking

9 A classmate says: "The rock cycle proves rocks can never really be destroyed — they just change shape." Is this statement accurate? What would be a more precise way to say it?
Partially correct — the atoms (matter) that make up rocks are recycled and not destroyed. But individual rocks definitely ARE destroyed — a granite boulder can be melted, dissolved by acid rain, or crushed into sediment. A more accurate statement: "The material rocks are made of gets recycled through the rock cycle — individual rocks are created and destroyed, but Earth's rock-forming material is conserved."
10 Mars has no liquid water at the surface, almost no atmosphere, and does not appear to have moving tectonic plates. Based on what you know about the rock cycle, predict which parts of the cycle would NOT work on Mars. Explain your reasoning.
No liquid water → no water-driven erosion or sediment transport, so the sedimentary branch of the rock cycle barely works. No plate tectonics → no subduction, so rocks don't get buried deep enough for most metamorphism, and there is little volcanic activity. Mars may still have some wind erosion and possibly a little internal heat, but its rock cycle is largely stalled compared to Earth's.
